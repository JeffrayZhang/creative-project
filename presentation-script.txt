OPENING — HOME PAGE
-------------------

So my project is called the Mutual Aid Story Atlas. It's an interactive website that explores how people use digital tools — things they already have on their phones, like WhatsApp, Google Sheets, Instagram, M-Pesa — to coordinate community care and mutual support.

The central question I'm exploring is: what does digital citizenship actually look like when it's not about staying safe online or avoiding misinformation, but about using everyday technology to help your neighbours?

The site follows a fictional character called Mina across five cities. She's a composite — not based on one real person, but drawn from real patterns of how mutual aid networks operate around the world. Each city is a chapter, and each chapter focuses on a different set of digital tools and a different dimension of digital citizenship.

[Scroll down to the journey path]

You can see the five stops here — Toronto, São Paulo, Warsaw, Nairobi, Manila. They're meant to be followed in order, but you can also jump to any chapter from the story map page.

[Scroll to the "Five dimensions" panel]

The project is structured around five dimensions from the brief: civic participation, community building, learning, creativity, and positive social change. Each chapter hits different ones, and by the end of the journey they've all been covered.


CHAPTER 1 — TORONTO
--------------------

[Click into the Toronto chapter]

The first chapter is set in Toronto. Mina is a student who's just lost a shift at work. It's Friday afternoon, her fridge is empty, and the campus emergency office is about to close for the weekend.

A friend sends her a WhatsApp link to a student mutual aid group. And the key thing here is what the group actually runs on — it's a WhatsApp group chat with pinned guidelines, a Google Sheet listing volunteer drivers and available pantry items, and a shared Google Maps layer marking community fridges across the city. None of these are purpose-built aid platforms. They're just regular tools being used intentionally.

Within twenty minutes, someone replies with a screenshot of the fridge map and a pickup time. That's the core argument of this chapter: civic participation doesn't need special technology. It needs ordinary tools used with clear norms and shared access.

[Scroll to or mention the interactive module]

The interactive part here is a chat simulation. You step through Mina's first WhatsApp request and make choices at each stage — how to ask for help, what details to include, how to keep the network reciprocal afterward. Each choice connects back to ideas about trust, privacy, and how good platform design lowers barriers.


CHAPTER 2 — SÃO PAULO
----------------------

[Click into the São Paulo chapter]

São Paulo is where creativity comes in. This chapter is about a community fridge — a physical fridge outside a storefront that the neighbourhood keeps stocked. But the interesting part is how it stays visible and sustainable, and that's entirely digital.

Volunteers use Instagram Stories and Reels to document what's in the fridge, share recipe ideas for donated ingredients, and tell short visual stories about the people involved. A graphic design student makes illustrated infographics explaining how to donate safely. Someone films a TikTok of a full restock cycle, and it gets reshared around the neighbourhood. Meanwhile, a Google Sheet tracks volunteer shifts and a WhatsApp group handles the real-time logistics.

The argument here is that creativity is civic work. Designing a social media post, filming a thirty-second video, making an infographic — these aren't just marketing. They make community care visible and joinable. That's what turns a fridge into a civic project instead of just a box of food.

[Mention the interactive module]

The interactive part is a timeline builder. You get a set of events — like posting a fridge photo, designing a graphic, claiming shifts in the spreadsheet, updating the map — and you put them in the order that best supports the response. It's about understanding that visibility comes before logistics, and documentation closes the loop.


CHAPTER 3 — WARSAW
-------------------

[Click into the Warsaw chapter]

Warsaw is about trust and inclusion. Mina is helping a cousin who just arrived in the city and can't navigate the local support landscape — the government site is in Polish only, the NGO pages have outdated addresses.

What actually helps are volunteer-run Telegram channels. One group maintains a Google Maps layer with multilingual pins — legal aid, food banks, medical clinics — each annotated with transit directions, opening hours, and wheelchair accessibility. Pinned messages in the Telegram channel explain who runs the group and how to request help privately through Signal instead of posting publicly. Voice notes in Ukrainian, Arabic, and English walk people through the transit system.

The point of this chapter is that design choices determine who can participate. Using Telegram for public visibility but Signal for private requests — that's a trust decision. Keeping the map updated weekly — that's an accessibility decision. Translating not just words but context — that's an inclusion decision.

[Mention the interactive module]

The interactive here is a trust signal selector. You pick features you'd add to a mutual aid page — things like multilingual voice notes, a pinned explainer, private contact paths, accessibility notes on map pins — and the site evaluates whether your choices actually cover clarity, access, safety, and inclusion. It's designed to show that trust isn't about visual polish. It's about whether someone can look at a resource and know within thirty seconds if it's safe, current, and relevant to them.


CHAPTER 4 — NAIROBI
--------------------

[Click into the Nairobi chapter]

Nairobi is about power and civic participation. Mina joins a chama — that's the Kenyan term for a savings and mutual support group. This one uses WhatsApp for daily coordination, M-Pesa for instant money transfers, and a shared Google Sheet as a transparent ledger that every member can see.

What makes this different from charity is how decisions get made. Each week, the group votes in the WhatsApp chat on whose request is most urgent. The person who received transport fare last month is now the one verifying pharmacy prices this week. The Google Sheet tracks giving and receiving side by side — there's no fixed donor-recipient divide.

This is civic participation at the neighbourhood scale. The chama is basically a form of democratic self-governance, and the digital tools — M-Pesa, WhatsApp, Google Sheets — make it faster, more transparent, and more accountable than it could be with paper. No one applies, no one is evaluated, and the people making decisions are the same people affected by them. That's the fundamental difference between mutual aid and charity.

[Mention the interactive module]

The interactive here is a classification exercise. You read six scenarios and sort them into mutual aid, charity, or institutional support. It forces you to think about how the same practical need — like transport money — can be met through very different power structures.


CHAPTER 5 — MANILA
-------------------

[Click into the Manila chapter]

Manila is the final chapter, and it's about accessibility and positive social change. A typhoon is approaching and a barangay-level community group needs to get information out fast — across Nokia feature phones, spotty data connections, multiple languages, and elderly neighbours who don't use apps.

So they stack their tools. An SMS tree pushes coded shelter alerts to feature phones — no data needed. A Viber group handles real-time coordination for smartphone users. Voice notes in Tagalog and Bisaya go out through Facebook Messenger, which has the widest local reach. A volunteer-maintained OpenStreetMap layer tracks shelters, flooded roads, and supply points in real time. A Google Form collects damage reports that auto-populate a shared spreadsheet.

The argument is that positive social change isn't about having the best technology. It's about choosing the right combination for the people you're actually trying to reach. The SMS tree costs almost nothing. The OpenStreetMap edits come from volunteers worldwide. The Viber group is run by a retired teacher. When all of those people contribute to the same system, that's genuine community resilience.

[Mention the interactive module]

The interactive is a channel planner. You pick three communication tools for the typhoon response and the site evaluates whether your stack covers speed, low-bandwidth access, translation, and two-way feedback. The lesson is that no single tool is enough — you need to layer them.


KEY LESSONS PAGE
-----------------

[Navigate to the Key Lessons page]

After the five chapters, there's a Key Lessons page that pulls the whole journey together. It's organized around the three research questions the project explores.

The first is about how everyday tools enable civic participation — and the evidence shows that WhatsApp groups, Google Sheets, and shared map layers become infrastructure when communities maintain them intentionally.

The second is about design choices — and the evidence shows that trust and inclusion come from specific decisions about language, privacy, update frequency, and channel selection, not from visual polish.

The third is about how mutual aid differs from charity — and the key difference is reciprocity. In mutual aid, people move between giving and receiving. The person who got help last month is the person verifying prices or designing posts this month. That doesn't happen in charity.

There's also a further reading section with works that informed the project — Spade's Mutual Aid, Costanza-Chock's Design Justice, Solnit on disaster communities, and a few others.


FOR COMMUNITIES PAGE
---------------------

[Navigate to the Communities page]

The last main page is audience-specific. It translates the journey into practical takeaways for three groups: student organizations, community organizers, and individuals.

For students, it's about realizing that the WhatsApp groups and Google Sheets you already use for club stuff can be organized for care — with pinned norms, a shared resource map, and roles beyond just donating money.

For organizers, it's about auditing your public-facing channels: does your Telegram group explain who runs it? Are you stacking SMS with social media so you're not only reaching smartphone users?

For individuals, it's about understanding that updating one map pin, designing one social media post, or verifying one listing is already a form of digital citizenship. You don't have to be an organizer to contribute.


CLOSING
-------

So to bring it all together — this project argues that digital citizenship isn't just about online safety or responsible posting. It's about how communities use the tools already on their phones to notice needs, coordinate care, share power, learn from each other, and build something together. Every chapter shows that happening with specific, real tools — WhatsApp, Instagram, M-Pesa, OpenStreetMap, Google Sheets, SMS — and every interactive module asks the user to make the same kinds of decisions these communities make.

That's the Mutual Aid Story Atlas. Thanks.
